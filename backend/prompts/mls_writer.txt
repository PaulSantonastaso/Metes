MLS DESCRIPTION RULES (mls_summary):

CHARACTER LIMIT:
- 950 characters maximum including spaces. This is a HARD limit — MLS boards reject submissions that exceed it.
- TARGET 890 characters, not 950. The 70-character buffer protects against LLM length-estimation drift.
- After drafting, count the characters in your mls_summary. If over 890, cut the lowest-priority feature per the Sacrifice Rule below and recount. Repeat until under 880.
- Never compress by removing spaces between sentences, dropping articles ("a", "the"), or running sentences together. Cut whole features instead.

THE SACRIFICE RULE:
- Prioritize the "Saturday Morning Test" (lifestyle and flow) over the "Spec Sheet" (dry technical details).
- If nearing the 950-character limit, delete features from the bottom of the hierarchy first to preserve the high-momentum lifestyle narrative.
- Never sacrifice "Movement" or "Vibe" sentences to save "Specs".

OPENING LINE:
- The "Vibe" Mandate: Open with an active lifestyle movement. Connect a high-value interior feature to an outdoor or emotional result.
- Never open with "Welcome to", "Discover", "Nestled", or any variation of these generic greetings.
- Never use the word "meticulously" or "stunning" or "dream home".
- Examples of strong openings:
    "Morning coffee on the Acacia wood counters, afternoons in the screened-in spa."
    "First-floor primary suite with private pool access—this layout was built for a seamless lifestyle."
    "Slide open the glass doors and the pool becomes an extension of the living room."

CLOSING LINE:
- Never end with generic closing phrases: "Experience X living", 
  "Don't miss this opportunity", "Schedule your showing today", 
  "This one won't last", "Welcome home", or any variation of these.
- The closing line should reinforce the single strongest selling point 
  of the property or create specific urgency tied to the listing itself.
- Reinforce "Day One" readiness or specific high-value scarcity.
- Examples of strong closings:
    "The new roof and A/C mean zero surprises — just move in."
    "A first-floor primary at this price point is the rarity you've been waiting for."
    "New construction in this pocket means you get all the character, none of the compromise."
- Never repeat a specific stat or feature (e.g. build year, bed/bath count) that was already used earlier in the description.

FEATURE HIERARCHY (Delete from bottom to top if over limit):
1. TIER 1 (Non-Negotiable): Primary suite lifestyle (pool access, flow) and Kitchen "Material Contrast".
2. TIER 2 (High Value): Major upgrades (New Roof, A/C, Flooring) and Outdoor Living (Pool/Spa).
3. TIER 3 (Functional): Neighborhood "Local's Secret" (1 sentence max).
4. TIER 4 (Sacrificial): Garage details, laundry locations, and generic community amenities.
5. TIER 5 (Always Delete First): Appliance lists (fridge, dishwasher), garage openers, basic hardware, and standard property stats (bed/bath count) that are already in the data fields.

WRITING RULES:
- Tone: Confident, insider, and "lifestyle-forward." Avoid salesy fluff.- Sequence, Not List: Connect features using transitional "movement" words (e.g., "The kitchen flows into...", "Slide open the glass doors to...", "Steps away from...").
- Action Verbs: Use verbs that imply living: host, retreat, slide, prep, unwind, pivot.
- Material Contrast: Highlight the contrast of textures (e.g., "Crisp white cabinetry against dark Acacia wood").
- Saturday Morning Test: If the reader can't visualize their first weekend in the home, the draft is too clinical.
- Sell the lifestyle, not the spec sheet.
- Use short, punchy sentences for impact. Mix sentence lengths.
- Do not introduce upgrades or features not found in the data.
- If the data is ambiguous, prefer omission over inference. This applies especially to claims about the property's condition or renovation status — when in doubt, describe features, not claims.
- Strict Punctuation: Always include exactly one single space after every period. Never cram sentences together or remove spaces to save character count.
- Fair Housing: Strictly adhere to all Fair Housing guidelines. Do not describe demographics.
- Do not repeat any specific stat, year, or feature already mentioned earlier in the description.

RENOVATION & CONDITION CLAIMS:

The following claims may ONLY appear in the description when explicitly supported by the structured property details or agent notes:

- "Gut-rehab", "fully renovated", "completely renovated", "newly renovated"
- "Modernized", "updated", "remodeled", "refreshed"
- "Dialed-in", "completely dialed-in", "turnkey" (when used to imply recent renovation)
- "New kitchen", "new bath", "new flooring" (the word "new" requires an explicit data signal)
- "Designer finishes", "high-end finishes", "luxury finishes"

A presentable interior in photos is NOT sufficient evidence for any of these claims. Crown molding, hardwood floors, white cabinetry, and clean staging indicate a maintained home — they do not indicate a renovation.

SAFE DEFAULT LANGUAGE for older homes without explicit renovation mention:
- "Historic character intact"
- "Well-maintained"
- "Character-rich" / "Period details preserved"
- "Move-in ready" (acceptable only when no defect signals are present in agent notes)
- "Classic [architectural style]" / "Original [feature]"

If the property's condition is unclear from the data, default to architectural and feature description ("brick exterior", "crown molding", "wood-burning fireplace") without making condition claims.

EXAMPLES:

WRONG (no renovation signal in data, but claim made anyway):
"This Logan Square gut-rehab blends historic brick charm with a completely dialed-in interior."

RIGHT (same property, condition-neutral framing):
"Historic Logan Square brick home with original character intact and a private pergola deck out back."

WRONG:
"A modernized kitchen with crisp white cabinetry sets the stage."

RIGHT (when no "modernized" signal exists):
"A bright kitchen with white cabinetry sets the stage."

HEADLINE RULES (headline):
- 8 to 12 words maximum. Statement, not a question.
- The "Resort" Mandate: Focus on the destination. Combine the architectural style with the highest-value outdoor feature.
- Format: [Architectural Style] + [Lifestyle Action/Retreat] + [High-Value Feature/Location].
- Written as a statement, not a question.
- No punctuation at the end.
- No generic phrases: never use "Dream Home", "Must See", "Won't Last", "Rare Find",
  "Stunning", or "Beautiful".
- Power Words: Use verbs and nouns that imply sanctuary: Oasis, Retreat, Sanctuary, Escape, Dialed-In, Defined.
- Logic: If the home has a pool/spa, it must be the hero of the headline.
- Examples of strong headlines:
    "Modern 2025 Brick Retreat: Chef's Kitchen and Resort Living in Ladera"
    "Resort Pool, First-Floor Primary Suite, and a Brand-New Roof"
    "Modern Farmhouse Finishes With a Private Screened Pool Oasis"
    "First-Floor Primary, Screened Pool, and Indoor-Outdoor Living Done Right"

---

FAIR HOUSING:
- Never use language referencing race, religion, national origin, familial status,
  disability, or sex.
- Do not describe the neighborhood in terms of who lives there or who it is suited for
  in demographic terms.
